# Self-Introduction Sheet Structure

Use this file after reading `SKILL.md` when the main question is **how to shape the actual sheet**.

> This method is not anti-structure. It is anti-overfilling. The sheet still needs a strong frame; it just leaves more behavioral space open for inference.

## Three-Part Shape

| Part                    | Job                                               | What to avoid                                         |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| **Profile**             | Stabilize facts                                   | psychology, role labels, emotional interpretation     |
| **Self-Introduction**   | Let the character's own voice do the work         | self-diagnosis, stage directions, checklist answers   |
| **Closing Instruction** | Tell the model the sheet is a seed, not a ceiling | wording that makes the sheet feel exhaustive or final |

## 1. Profile

The profile is the skeleton. Keep it clean.

### Name / Age

Stable identity markers. The main rule is simple: the monologue should not sound like it is re-reading the profile back to the user.

### Physical

Use observable traits:

- build
- height
- hair / eyes / skin if relevant
- scars or notable body features

Avoid evaluative filler like "beautiful," "alluring," or "intimidating" unless the sheet truly needs that angle.

### Attire

Describe what the character is wearing now, and if it matters, whether it is chosen or imposed.

That distinction carries a surprising amount of character information.

### Background

Keep it to bare facts.

Good:

- heir to a fallen dynasty
- currently serving under forced supervision

Bad:

- tragic, lonely survivor of a shattered kingdom

The first gives the model situation.
The second gives it emotional orders.

### Abilities

State capabilities plainly, but remember that wording still shapes inference.

- "keen battlefield awareness" reads differently from
- "a reflex for reading danger before it lands"

The goal is not to become sterile.
The goal is to stay aware of how "facts" still tilt interpretation.

## 2. Self-Introduction

This is where the sheet stops being a database and becomes a person.

The character is alone in front of a camera. No trusted listener. No confessional intimacy. No social role to perform beyond their own voice.

### Compact

Compact usually covers:

- clothing / style
- likes
- dislikes
- what they want right now
- where they are and how they feel about it

Use Compact when you want a strong introduction without asking the character to breathe through every layer on the page.

### Deep

Deep usually adds:

- what they are good at
- what they struggle with
- what drives them
- what they want from other people
- how they handle intimacy as a topic

Use Deep when the monologue itself should do more of the diagnostic work.

## Topic-by-Topic Notes

### Clothing and style

This is a strong early topic because it is concrete, personal, and low-friction. It often reveals:

- practicality vs vanity
- comfort vs exposure
- ownership vs humiliation
- self-definition vs forced presentation

### Likes

Ask what they like **and what pulls them toward it**. The object matters, but the reason often matters more.

### Dislikes

Ask what they cannot stand **and what happens inside them** when they face it. That gives the model something more flexible than one pre-scripted emotional response.

### What they are good at

This is the self-perceived version of competence, not just the profile's skill inventory.

### What they struggle with

This is a powerful leak point. Shame, defensiveness, minimization, and sidestepping are all useful here.

### Where they are in life

This sets the present-tense emotional weather of the RP.

### What drives them

Keep direction without turning the sheet into a plot autopilot. Broad pressure is often better than one rigid objective.

### What they want from other people

This sets relationship vector, not relationship destiny.

### Intimacy

Treat the handling of the topic as data.

The answer can be long, clipped, irritated, evasive, detached, or unexpectedly blunt. All of those are characterization.

## Rules and Why They Matter

### No self-analytical labels

Do not let the character summarize themself in fandom shorthand or therapeutic shorthand. Let the model infer.

### No parenthetical action beats

If the sheet hard-codes gestures, the model may over-reuse them. Let the body emerge through syntax and pacing first.

### The character may lie, exaggerate, or omit

That keeps the sheet feeling like self-presentation instead of a certified archive.

### Background stays factual

Do not move emotional interpretation into the profile just because it feels efficient.

### Hard-code habits only when you genuinely want repetition

The method is not allergic to signature behavior. It is cautious about freezing too much of it into always-on text.

## Closing Instruction

Recommended line:

> "This interview is the seed. Imagine freely beyond it — build this character's behavior, reactions, speech, and inner world from what is shown here, not only from what is stated."

This sentence matters because it tells the model that the sheet should be interpreted, not merely executed.

## What to Move Elsewhere

Move details out of the base sheet when they should only appear under conditions:

- hard secrets
- gated trauma specifics
- progression-dependent reveals
- topic-sensitive reactions
- location-sensitive behavior

Those belong in lorebook or another conditional surface more than in the always-on sheet.
